The Vision Turing Test

Computer Vision · Classification
Accuracy: 92.3% AUC: ~97% 15k Images

Overview

Given the rapid adoption of generative AI, this project explores methods for distinguishing AI-generated images from human-generated images. Images were sourced from two Hugging Face repositories and the open-source Open Images dataset from Google Research — a total of 15,000 images, evenly split between human and AI generated.

Three traditional machine learning models were pitted against the EfficientNetB0 CNN to determine the best approach for generative AI detection. The paper on findings and the full code notebook are included in the repository, with the CNN training separated into its own notebook for easy GPU kernel switching.

Key Findings

  • Best performing model: a tuned Random Forest trained on engineered image features (edge density, edge entropy, height, width, color statistics). Accuracy was 92.3% with an AUC of ~97%.
  • AI images showed a wider range across RGB channels, suggesting they may be more vibrant than human-generated images.
  • AI images showed lower contrast — their edges appeared flatter and more pronounced, a signal the engineered features could reliably capture.
  • EfficientNetB0 underperformed the feature-engineered Random Forest, suggesting that hand-crafted spectral and edge features are more discriminative than learned CNN representations for this specific task.

Use Cases

  • Social media platforms can use this model to flag AI-generated images, helping maintain content authenticity and reduce the spread of synthetic misinformation.
  • Fact-checkers and investigative journalists can run suspect images through the classifier as one signal in verifying whether visual evidence in a story has been manipulated or fabricated.
  • Digital forensics and legal teams can use the model’s feature-based approach to establish a basis for challenging image authenticity in court.
